# Joinery Reference

## Primary Methods (Available with Current Tools)

### Pocket Screws (Kreg)
**Best for:** Cabinet carcass assembly, face frames, quick strong joints
- Drill angled pocket holes, drive self-tapping screws
- Strong, fast, requires no clamping time
- Joint is hidden on inside/back of assembly
- **Settings:** Match drill depth and screw length to material thickness
- **Tips:**
  - Use fine-thread screws for hardwood, coarse for softwood/plywood
  - Minimum 3/4" from edge of material
  - Pre-clamp pieces to prevent shifting during driving

### Dados and Rabbets (Router)
**Best for:** Shelf joints, back panel capture, drawer boxes
- **Dado:** Groove cut across the grain to receive another piece
- **Rabbet:** Step cut along the edge (for back panels, box joints)
- Cut with router + straight bit + straightedge guide
- **Tips:**
  - Test fit on scrap — plywood is rarely exactly 3/4"
  - Use 23/32" bit for 3/4" plywood dados (or make two passes)
  - Dado depth: 1/4" to 1/3" of material thickness
  - Rabbet depth for back panels: 1/4" (to accept 1/4" back)

### Butt Joints (Glue + Fasteners)
**Best for:** Simple boxes, utility shelving, quick assemblies
- Glue + brad nails, screws, or pocket screws
- Weakest joint type — reinforce with glue and mechanical fasteners
- **Tips:** Ensure edges are flat and square for good glue surface

### Biscuit Joints
**Best for:** Edge-to-edge glue-ups, alignment aid
- Requires biscuit joiner (if you acquire one)
- Good for aligning panels during glue-up
- Not a primary structural joint

## Adhesives

### Wood Glue (Titebond II)
- **Open time:** ~5 minutes
- **Clamp time:** 30-60 minutes
- **Full cure:** 24 hours
- **Water resistance:** Moderate (Type II)
- **Best for:** All interior wood-to-wood joints
- **Tip:** Thin, even coat on both surfaces. Squeeze-out means enough glue.

### Titebond III (if needed)
- **Water resistance:** Waterproof (Type III)
- **Use when:** Outdoor furniture, bathroom cabinets, high-moisture environments

### Construction Adhesive
- **Use:** Mounting toe kicks, securing countertop build-ups, panel bonding
- **Not a substitute** for wood glue in joinery

## Joint Strength Ranking (for cabinets)
1. Dado + glue (strongest shelf joint)
2. Pocket screws + glue (fast and strong)
3. Dowels + glue (strong but slower, requires jig)
4. Biscuits + glue (primarily for alignment)
5. Butt joint + glue + nails (weakest, utility only)

## Tips
- **Always dry-fit** before gluing — check for square, verify alignment
- **Glue + mechanical fastener** is always stronger than either alone
- **Square = strong:** An out-of-square assembly creates stress points
- **Clamping pressure:** Even pressure matters more than maximum pressure
